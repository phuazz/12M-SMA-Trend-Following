Why It Works in Crises

The 12M SMA strategy sidesteps the worst of long, sustained bear markets by moving to T-bills when the index falls below its trailing average. It won't catch the exact top or bottom — the signal is intentionally slow — but in protracted declines like the Great Depression, GFC, and dot-com bust, the lag is a feature, not a bug.

Where It Doesn't Work

Short, sharp crashes like COVID (34 days peak to trough) and V-shaped recoveries produce whipsaw losses. The signal exits late and re-enters late, missing the recovery bounce. The 12M SMA is designed for sustained bear markets, not flash crashes.

The Rule

At the end of each month, check where the S&P 500 price stands relative to its 12-month simple moving average:

Price > 12M SMA → Stay invested in the S&P 500
Price ≤ 12M SMA → Move to T-bills (cash equivalent)

Rebalance monthly. That's the entire strategy.

Why 12 Months?

The 12-month (~200-day) moving average is the most widely studied trend-following signal in equity markets. Shorter lookbacks (3M, 6M) produce more frequent signals and higher transaction costs with marginal improvement. Longer lookbacks (18M, 24M) are too slow to respond to regime changes. The 12M window balances signal reliability against responsiveness.

Signal Flow
Monthly Decision Process
① End of month → observe S&P 500 closing price
② Compute 12-month SMA of closing prices
③ If Price > SMA → allocate 100% to S&P 500
④ If Price ≤ SMA → allocate 100% to T-bills
⑤ Execute at next month's open
⑥ Repeat

The Pattern

The 12M SMA outperforms in bad decades (1930s, 1970s, 2000s) and underperforms in strong bull markets (1950s, 1980s, 1990s, 2010s). You give up upside in exchange for protection when it matters most.
